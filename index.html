ABOUT DFR Deployment Planner
What this tool does

Plans where to put DroneBox stations.

Drone as First Responder ("DFR") programs put automated drones inside small dock-stations across a city. When an emergency call comes in, the nearest drone launches automatically and arrives on scene typically before the patrol car. This tool decides how many DroneBox stations you need, and where to put them, so the response time meets your KPI for every kind of incident.

Each yellow dot is a DroneBox. Each cyan circle is the area one DroneBox can reach within the response-time KPI. Pink dots are past incidents — they tell the algorithm where demand is.

How you use it

Three steps. About five minutes.

1
Draw the operational area. A polygon on the map showing where the DFR program will run. Add no-fly zones (palace approaches, airports) and no-deploy zones (locations where you can't install a DroneBox).
2
Set the incident profile. Define categories like Very Critical / Critical / Routine. Each gets its own response-time KPI (45s, 60s, 120s). Then either generate synthetic incidents from a distribution, or upload your real historical incident data.
3
Compute. The algorithm finds where to place the stations to cover the most incidents while meeting KPI for every category. You get a map, a station list with coordinates, and a defensible report.
The output is a starting point for site-by-site engineering studies — not a final deployment plan. Each recommended site still needs ground assessment for power, roof access, GCAA approval, and line-of-sight to the airspace.
How it calculates

Two questions: where, and how many.

Where to place stations
A greedy maximum-coverage algorithm: place the first station where it covers the most incidents, the next where it covers the most remaining incidents, and so on. Each placement also considers no-fly zones and flight-path blockage.
How many drones per site
A time-aware simulation replays every incident on the timeline. When two calls overlap, more than one drone is needed. The simulation grows the unit count at each site until every category hits its target service level.

When the third call (red) arrives while both drones are still busy, that's a missed KPI. The simulation counts these to decide how many drone units are really needed at each station.

What it assumes

Honest about what's modelled.

The math is defensible for sizing decisions, but it's a planning tool, not a flight simulator. Here's what's in the model and what isn't:

Modelled honestly
Per-category response-time KPIs · launch overhead (dock-open + sensor warmup + climb) · concurrent-call collisions · no-fly zone avoidance · standoff points · realistic drone specs from operator-reported data.
Simplified
Straight-line flight paths (real routing adds 10–25% time) · constant mission duration per category · 100% drone availability · no weather/dust downtime · single dispatch policy (nearest available).
Use the output as a starting point. For procurement defensibility, calibrate the model against your actual DroneBox operational data — real flight logs trump manufacturer marketing every time.
How effective is it?

Within 5–15% of optimal.

The greedy algorithm produces near-optimal placement for typical configurations. Compared to deploying without a planning tool, it removes guesswork on three measurable axes:

①
Right number of stations. Avoids both over-procurement (wasting budget on stations whose marginal coverage is near zero) and under-procurement (deploying too few and missing KPI on critical incidents).
②
Right placement. The map output is coordinate-precise. Each site is selected to maximize coverage given the surrounding incident density.
③
Right unit count per station. Concurrency simulation prevents the "all drones busy" failure mode that ground-up planning often misses.
The output PDF gives leadership a one-page defensible artifact: map, station list with coordinates, coverage statistics, and the marginal-value curves that justify why eight stations and not nine.
Why we built it

Because this work shouldn't start from scratch.

Dubai Police's Unmanned Aerial Systems Center has spent years building one of the world's first urban DFR programs. The DroneBox network — concept in 2018, launch at Expo 2020, 4,377 incidents supported by 2024 — exists because someone had to figure out the answers to "how many" and "where" and "what platform" the first time.

This tool encodes what we learned. Future agencies building DFR programs — police, civil defense, search-and-rescue, emergency medical, port authorities — shouldn't have to derive the math again. They should be able to draw their city, upload last year's incident log, and get a defensible plan in five minutes.

The goal isn't to sell anything. It's to make DFR deployment boring and predictable, so the focus can shift to what actually matters: response times, lives saved, and the trust citizens place in their first responders.
